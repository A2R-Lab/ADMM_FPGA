# Research History: Hardware-Algorithm Co-Design for 1kHz FPGA-MPC

This document details the technical journey and co-design methodologies used to upgrade the ADMM-based MPC solver from its ICRA 2026 baseline (500 Hz) to a robust 1 kHz architecture.

## 1. The Baseline Bottleneck (ICRA 2026)
The initial architecture successfully demonstrated 500 Hz constrained control on a 35g quadrotor. However, attempting to scale to 1 kHz exposed a fundamental hardware limitation:
*   **The "Hidden" Timing Failure:** While the design functioned in lab conditions, static timing analysis revealed a Worst Negative Slack (WNS) of -0.119 ns at 100 MHz.
*   **Routing Congestion:** The HLS compiler aggressively unrolled logic to maximize parallelism. This created a monolithic, highly entangled logic structure that consumed ~80% of the Artix-7 100T's LUTs. The Vivado router could not physically route the wires fast enough to meet the 10 ns clock period, resulting in electrical instability.
*   **The Co-Design Challenge:** To hit 1 kHz (a 1.0 ms hard deadline, including 0.1 ms SPI overhead), we needed to reduce solver latency to < 0.9 ms. However, simply "unrolling more loops" or "overclocking to 125 MHz" was impossible because the FPGA's routing resources were already congested and failing timing.

## 2. Hardware-Algorithm Co-Design Methodology
To break this barrier, we had to move beyond algorithmic improvements and actively co-design the underlying hardware architecture generated by Vitis HLS. We mapped the algorithmic operations to specific FPGA primitives (DSPs vs. LUTs) to alleviate routing congestion.

### Phase A: The `II=1` Trap and the Pipeline Penalty
Initially, HLS attempted to pipeline the dynamic stage loops with an Initiation Interval of 1 (`II=1`).
*   **Discovery:** Forcing `II=1` on the complex matrix-vector products created massive data dependencies. HLS attempted to resolve this by instantiating huge multiplexers in LUTs rather than using efficient distributed registers.
*   **Counter-intuitive Fix:** By explicitly relaxing the pipeline constraint to `II=4`, the HLS scheduler was given "breathing room." This actually *decreased* total latency by 15% because the schedule was more efficient, though it caused area to explode to 125% LUT utilization as HLS aggressively instantiated multipliers.

### Phase B: Forcing DSP Reuse via Functional Isolation
To combat the 125% LUT explosion, we needed to force the algorithm to stop synthesizing logic in LUTs and instead reuse the FPGA's dedicated DSP slices.
*   **Algorithmic Refactoring:** We extracted the innermost mathematical operations (the sparse matrix-vector products of the dynamic stage) into a strict, non-inlined static function: `dynamic_stage_compute`.
*   **Hardware Allocation:** We applied the `#pragma HLS ALLOCATION function instances=dynamic_stage_compute limit=1` directive.
*   **Result:** This was a "sledgehammer" approach. It forced the entire solver to time-multiplex a single physical compute engine. LUT usage plummeted from 125% down to a highly routable **69%**, proving we could control the area. However, latency spiked to 2.5 ms.

### Phase C: Finding the Pareto Optimal "4-Engine" Architecture
With area under control, we scaled the architecture back up to find the exact intersection of timing-closure and 1 kHz throughput.
*   **Scaling Parallelism:** We increased the allocation limit to 4 (`limit=4`) and unrolled the caller loop (`UNROLL factor=4`).
*   **The Architecture:** The algorithm was now mapped to a **4-Engine Shared Resource Architecture**. Instead of a monolithic block of entangled logic, the FPGA instantiated exactly 4 independent compute cores that efficiently shared data.
*   **Substitution Tuning:** To shave the final microseconds, the forward and backward substitution loops were unrolled by a factor of 2 (`UNROLL factor=2`), parallelizing the banded matrix solves.

## 3. Final Verification and Results
This strict hardware-algorithm co-design approach yielded a fundamentally superior FPGA implementation.

## 4. Conclusion for the Paper Narrative
The transition to 1 kHz was not achieved by raw algorithmic efficiency or brute-force overclocking, but through deep HW/SW co-design. By identifying routing congestion as the primary bottleneck, we refactored the ADMM algorithm to map onto a custom 4-engine shared-resource architecture. This approach deliberately constrained HLS loop unrolling and forced DSP reuse, transforming a congested, timing-failing design into a formally verified, high-speed controller capable of deterministic 1 kHz flight control on the same resource-constrained Artix-7 platform.
